Project Overview

The Future Resilient Communities Research Project is a multi-disciplinary research project funded by the National Science Foundation (NSF) Smart and Connected Communities (S&CC) program. The project aims to develop a novel framework for enhancing the resilience of communities to natural and human-made disasters through the integration of advanced communication technologies, data analytics, and community engagement.

Research Team

The project brings together a diverse team of researchers from the fields of civil and industrial engineering, computer science, and social sciences to address the complex challenges of building resilient communities in the face of increasing disaster risks. The research team is collaborating with local communities to co-develop and implement innovative solutions that leverage the latest advances in technology and data science to enhance community resilience.

This project foundationally integrates social, technical and community partners by engaging all of them in all phases of the work.The research project proposes 4 thrusts ( Thrust 1, Thrust 2, Thrust 3, and Thrust 4.) to answer various research questions, and each thrust will be led by one or two PI/Co-PI(s) on the team and will engage all team members.

Thrust 1 - Robust communications solutions

Develop robust off-grid community-based communication networks that can function during internet failure.

Answer questions: How can we design community communications infrastructure to be robust to disasters? How can we best build computing services that leverage community-held infrastructure during a disaster?

Thrust 2 - Building community social networks

Conduct random sample surveys to collect information about people's sharing behavior and their social ties within a community, and develop novel models that infer social ties for the entire community, to generates an initial social network snapshot for a community.

Answer questions: Can we obtain information on community-based social networks without actually collecting detailed social tie data? If yes, how to do it?

Thrust 3 - Design for information-resource sharing

Understand how social network structures affect information-resource sharing, as well as formulate strategies that will lead to efficient information-resource sharing.

Answer questions: How to disseminate information efficiently during a disaster?

Thrust 4 - Dynamic "Map your neighborhood"

Integrate the results of Thrusts 1-3 and use them to co-design and pilot-test applications with two communities.

Answer questions: How does the proposed dynamic map-your-neighborhood program better support robust communication and efficient information-resource sharing during a disaster?

History

This section provides a detailed timeline of significant events and milestones in the development of our community resilience projects.

2016
In 2016, Cynthia Chen and Dan Abramson began to develop a collaborative interest in researching social-technical systems resilience at the community scale as it might become expressed in a major disruption to communications and transportation. Cynthia had been working on disaster resilience by both developing novel resilience indices with big data and modeling the failure propagation process on interdependent infrastructure systems. Dan had been working both internationally and locally for ten years on earthquake and tsunami recovery and hazards mitigation planning. The two felt that a community-based focus was important. They undertook to co-advise an incoming PhD student in Urban Design and Planning, Katherine Idziorek, to work on this topic with them. With degrees in both architectural and urban design and planning, and 8 years of professional practice in community engagement for urban design and transportation projects, Katie was well-qualified to contribute to this work.
April 2017
April 2017, John Temple and Roger Roffman (both of Laurelhurst residents and Roger is a UW emeritus professor) approached Cynthia, with a proposal of having a student from UW to conduct a community-based earthquake preparedness project in Laurelhurst. Cynthia approached Dan and Katie about the idea, and they met with John to discuss how their research and professional experience might serve the Laurelhurst effort.
June 2017
June 2017, UW team (Katie and Cynthia) met with Laurelhurst LEAP leadership including John Temple and Roger Roffman.

2017
2017 is also the first year for TOMNET funding and Cynthia put in a proposal for "Attitudes and Trust in Leveraging Integrated Sociotechnical Systems for Enhancing Community Adaptive Capacity Phase I". This is the first funded project for Katie's dissertation. TOMNET funding continued for Phase II, III, and IV in 2021, when Katie finished her dissertation. and supported her to conduct household surveys on attitudes towards local resource and information sharing given social relationships and disruptions to transportation. With TOMNET funding, 2017 is the first year that our focus on community-based peer-to-peer resource and information sharing started.
2017-2018
During the 2017-2018 academic year, Dan, Cynthia and Katie also developed a proposal for Bullitt Foundation funding to work with both Laurelhurst and Westport, a small rural community on the Washington coast that had taken remarkable steps to prepare for a catastrophic tsunami, by rebuilding its elementary school as a tsunami vertical evacuation structure. The successful proposal supported the mounting of an applied practical planning studio course and community workshops to explore the need for and potential of new communications infrastructure to enhance resilience in the face of these hazards.
2021
In 2021, following Katie's graduation, Cynthia and Dan received Global Innovation Fund support to explore international comparative study of this topic with partners in Japan. The GIF study built capacity for US-Japan collaboration on community-based resilience work.
2020-2022
NSF-JST planning project came in led by Cynthia Chen between 2020 and 2022. Dan served as Co-PI with two new other Co-PIs, Payman Arahshahi and Sreeram Kannan of ECE UW. The project continued analysis of the community survey results, in combination with a comparative survey in Nagoya, Japan, and exploring further the requirements of physical communications infrastructure and software design for P2P resource and information sharing.

NSF LEAP-HI funding on creating adaptable societies led by Cynthia Chen and Dan Abramson serves as a co-PI. The project focused on how to create adaptable systems that will work together. NSF-JST and NSF LEAP-HI funding and TOMNET funding also supported a new Urban Design and Planning student, Cristina Cano-Calhoun, for her MUP thesis to conduct qualitative interviews of partner community stakeholders for P2P sharing.
2023-2027
2023 - 2027, current NSF SCC project led by Cynthia Chen. We brought in new Co-PIs: Kurtis, Shuai and Tyler.
